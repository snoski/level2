Average stock returns by month: Are they really what they seem?

The next time you see a statistic in the news, please be skeptical.

A favorite talking point

Watch CNBC long enough (or scroll through a few financial tweets), and you'll eventually hear a talking head say something like, "You know...September is the worst month on average for the stock market." And then they'll show a graph like this:

But what does that statistic really mean?

Should investors be afraid of September? The answer is, as with many things, far more complicated than such a simple statistic can convey. Consider the following graph, which adds only one additional statistic but provides a lot more information:

The mean, which is the simple arithmetic average that was presented in the first graph above, is far more negative for September than its median, which is the middle value of the data set. This tells us that there are large negative outliers in the data for September that make the simple average appear far worse than September actually usually is.

Furthermore, in the first graph, the mean for May makes it look like it is the 3rd worst month, when in reality, it is actually a little better than the average month! While these are interesting revelations, I hope you don't stop there but instead ask the question...

Well, how much variation is there in this data?

While averages help to explain where the CENTER of the data is, they do nothing at all to explain how much the data varies AROUND that center. A histogram is a great tool for visualizing the distribution of a data set. Each bar in the charts below is a "bucket" that contains all the monthly returns in a 2.5% range. So for instance, the first bar to the left of the black line is the count of all monthly returns that have occurred for that month from -2.5% to 0%. The bar to the right of the black line represents the count of occurrences from 0% to 2.5%, then 2.5% to 5% and so on. Take a look at September and think about what you see. Then take a minute to click through the other months, and think about what you see.

You can click the arrows below to cycle through the months, or you can click the image itself to toggle between displaying one month at a time or all months simultaneously.

What did you notice? Did you see how the values are mostly clumped around the center for all of the months? Did you notice any extreme values in any months? How about September? Do you see how an outlier might be affecting the data? What did you think about the general shape of September as compared to the other months? Was it substantially different than the other months or mostly similar?

Good! It's important to recognize when outliers might be skewing the data in a way that that would be unrealistic to rely upon when you're forming expectations about what is likely to happen. By definition, outliers have a very low probability of occurring. In the case of September, it recorded the single largest monthly loss in the history of the stock market since 1927.
