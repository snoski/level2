Average stock returns by month: Are they really what they seem?

The next time you see a statistic in the news, please be skeptical.

A favorite talking point

Watch CNBC long enough (or scroll through a few financial tweets), and you'll eventually hear a talking head say something like, "You know...September is the worst month on average for the stock market." And then they'll show a graph like this:

But what does that statistic really mean?

Should investors be afraid of September? The answer is, as with many things, far more complicated than such a simple statistic can convey. Consider the following graph, which adds only one additional statistic but provides a lot more information:

The mean, which is the simple arithmetic average that was presented in the first graph above, is far more negative for September than its median, which is the middle value of the data set. This gives us our first hint what is actually happening in the data for September! (If you don't know yet, please read on.)

Similarly, in the first graph for May, the mean made it look like it is the 3rd worst month, but according to the median, it is actually a little better than the average month! While these are interesting revelations, I hope you don't stop there but instead ask the question after the fold.

Well, how much variation is there in this data?

While averages help to explain where the CENTER of the data is, they do nothing at all to explain how much the data varies AROUND that center. A histogram is a great tool for visualizing the distribution of a data set. Each bar in the charts below is a "bucket" that contains all the monthly returns in a 2.5% range. So for instance, the first bar to the left of the black line is the count of all monthly returns that have occurred for that month from -2.5% to 0%. The bar to the right of the black line represents the count of occurrences from 0% to 2.5%, then 2.5% to 5% and so on. Take a look at September and think about what you see. Then take a minute to click through the other months, and think about what you see.

You can click the arrows below to cycle through the months, or you can click the image itself to toggle between displaying one month at a time or all months simultaneously.

What did you notice? Did you see how the values are mostly clumped around the center for all of the months? Did you notice any extreme values in any months? How about September? Do you see how an outlier might be affecting the data? What did you think about the general shape of September as compared to the other months? Was it substantially different than the other months or mostly similar?

Good! It's important to recognize when outliers might be skewing the data in a way that that would be unrealistic to rely upon when you're forming expectations about what is likely to happen. By definition, outliers have a very low probability of occurring.

In the case of September, the largest monthly crash in the history of the stock market since 1927 was recorded in 1931 amidst the Great Depression. This occurrence skews the data in such a way that the mean (the simple arithmetic average) is heavily influenced by this large negative value.

But just using common sense, should investors be afraid that a Great Depression return is a likely outcome for any given September? I'll tell you the answer: no. So why are people using a statistic that is heavily influenced by that highly improbable number?

Let's see what the data looks like without those outliers!

As you probably noticed earlier, the return distributions for all 12 months are clumped around zero with values becoming increasingly less probable as we get further away from zero. The chart below shows the cumulative percentage of monthly returns that have an absolute value less than a particular percentage. This is just a fancy way of saying "is between the negative and positive values of that percentage." This is very close to the 95% of occurences that can be expected to fall within 2 standard deviations in a "normal distribution." Investors taking their advice from the talking heads on CNBC might have decided to sit on the sidelines in September when there's actually a reaonable chance the stock market will go up!
